The Memory Supercycle: who really wins the HBM build-out

DRAM economics, the high-bandwidth-memory oligopoly, and the ASX-adjacent ways to own the squeeze. A research-grade map of the trade of the decade.

By Livewire Research · 12 June 2026 · 25 min read · Data appendix included

Photo illustration: Livewire · Source: Getty

Every supercycle begins as a shortage nobody believes. In 1999 it was fibre. In 2007 it was iron ore. In 2026 it is high-bandwidth memory — the stacked DRAM that feeds every accelerator in every data centre now being poured, raised and energised across the world's grids. The AI build-out gets the headlines; the memory squeeze pays the dividends.

The arithmetic is brutal and simple. An AI accelerator carries six to eight HBM stacks. Each stack consumes roughly three times the wafer area of the commodity DRAM it displaced — and yields worse. Every point of share the AI complex takes from conventional memory removes supply the rest of the economy still needs. Three companies control effectively all of it.

That oligopoly — SK Hynix, Samsung and Micron — has spent two decades destroying its own pricing power with ill-timed capacity races. The discipline this cycle is different, and the reason is structural: HBM is sold on multi-year contracts, qualified part-by-part into each accelerator generation. Switching costs have arrived in a commodity industry. The result is the most interesting margin story in global hardware — and almost nobody on the ASX is positioned for it.
